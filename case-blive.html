No. 04 SHIPPED 2024 — 2025 · EV MOBILITY

BLive EZY

// EV rental + fleet management + IoT telematics platform

E2EBA + PM ownership
3portals · admin · FO · rider
IoTlive telematics

Role

Business Analyst and Project Manager. Owned discovery, requirements, workflow definition, sprint delivery, and stakeholder reporting from kickoff through launch. The single point of accountability between client, design, and engineering.

Stack

  • Figma
  • GitHub
  • SQL
  • Excel
  • JIRA

Context

One product, two completely different users

BLive EZY had to serve two audiences whose needs barely overlap: a consumer renting an EV for a few hours, and a fleet operator managing a few hundred vehicles. Same product, same database — but the interface, the priorities, and even the language are different in each surface.

The risk on a brief like this is to ship something that's "fine for both" and great for neither. The early scoping work was about finding the actual seam.

Approach

Define the actor's happy path before the admin's

I sequenced the build around the rental customer first, then layered the fleet-ops surface on top of the working data model. The rental customer is the revenue source — if their flow breaks, nothing else matters. The fleet-ops surface is the leverage layer — important, but downstream of a working customer experience.

  • Discovery interviews with both rental customers and fleet operators. Two completely different problem statements emerged from the same product.
  • Workflow specs for: rider onboarding, eKYC verification, vehicle allocation, rental plans, payment reconciliation and dues tracking, return + handoff, fleet rebalancing, maintenance routing.
  • IoT telematics specs for: live vehicle tracking, geo-fencing alerts, remote immobilization, SOS alerts, and per-vehicle utilisation monitoring.
  • Reporting specs for the ops side: deployed vehicles, booking conversion, cancellations, overdue dues, payment failures, and fleet utilisation.
  • Sprint cadence: two-week sprints. Customer surface shipped first; ops surface in parallel once the data model stabilised.

Architecture

Two surfaces, one truth

                    [ shared data model ]
                    ↑             ↑
                vehicles · users · bookings · maintenance
                    ↑             ↑
       [ Customer rental ]      [ Fleet ops console ]
       // search · book · pay     // utilisation · maintenance
       //   verify · drive        //   rebalancing · revenue

The design call I'm proudest of: the rental customer never sees an admin flag, and the fleet operator never sees a "favourites" button. Same database, two carefully shaped windows into it. The ops console doesn't try to be a dashboard for the rental side, and the rental side doesn't try to be helpful in admin ways.

A platform with two users isn't one product with two coats of paint. It's two products that happen to share a backend — and the discipline is in keeping each interface honest to its actual user.

Outcome

Both surfaces shipped, both running

Customer-side bookings flow end-to-end: search, ID/license capture, payment, vehicle handoff, return. Ops console gives fleet managers a live view of utilisation, idle time, maintenance backlog, and vehicle-level revenue. The product is in market.

The data model held up: when ops needed a new aggregation later in the build, it was a query, not a migration. That's the BA work paying back — decisions made during specification stayed cheap to extend.

What I'd do differently

Sit with the fleet operator longer at discovery

I underestimated how much of the ops surface lives in non-obvious workflows: a swap that's not a maintenance event but isn't a normal handoff either; the difference between a vehicle that's "down" vs "deprioritised". Two more days of discovery on the fleet side would have surfaced these earlier and trimmed late-stage iteration.
